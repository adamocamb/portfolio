Machined Aluminum Rear Bike Rack

An in-progress bikepacking rack for my own gravel bike, designed to carry overnight gear without feeling fragile, sloppy, or overbuilt.

Project Goal

The goal is to build a bikepacking rack that I can use and enjoy on rough gravel. It needs to carry a compact rear bag securely, survive dynamic loading, stay reasonably light, avoid annoying bag sway, and be cheap enough that I can prototype without treating every design decision like a production tooling investment.

This is also a useful portfolio project because it is not just a CAD model. It forces the same product-development loop I use professionally: requirements, tradeoffs, load paths, interfaces, manufacturing limits, fastener details, test planning, and iteration after use.

Concept Tradeoffs

I looked at several ways to make the rack. A welded steel-tube rack is attractive because steel has good fatigue behavior, the weight difference could be small if the tubes are efficient, and we have welding capability. The downsides are material cost, bigger bend radii with the available 1/2 in tubing, harder to bolt accessory mounts on round tube, and the fact that welds are common failure points on racks if the design or execution is not right (especially when I'm welding).

I also considered steel tubing with machined aluminum hardware, but that creates extra joining problems. Bolting or bonding steel to aluminum adds corrosion concerns unless the materials are isolated, and it would still leave me with a more complicated assembly.

My preferred design would be 5052 aluminum bar bent in a U-shaped for the uprights. That would reduce bolted corner joints and simplify the rack. The issue is that buying the material with shipping pushes the cost up, and bending a roughly 36 in long bar into a controlled U shape is not something I currently have easy access to. I'm confident I could build a bender, but that adds cost and delays the prototype.

Current Direction

For the first build, I am using 6061 aluminum from the machine shop, minimizing bends, and machining brackets so the rack can bolt together instead of depending on tight 90-degree bends or welds. This is not the design I would choose for mass production, but it is the fastest and cheapest way to test the loading, fit, and ride behavior on my own bike. And I want this in time for an upcoming trip!

The rack will be sized around my frame so the bike itself helps form a structural triangle. That lets me use less material in the rack instead of making a fully self-supporting structure. The trade-off is it's not overly adjustable if I get a new bike.

Several details still need careful design: features at the upper bracket that carry side load without relying only on aluminum threads and M5 bolts, removable fastener retention for rough gravel, and a spot to carry spare M5 bolts.

Analysis and Validation Plan

The first weak-link question is the bike interface. I have a note that a typical M5 frame mount may be limited to around 50 lb, which would imply less than about 16 lb per side with a 3x safety factor. I need to verify that number before building the rest of the rack, because there is no point designing the structure around loads the frame mounts cannot support.

  • Confirm the allowable load for the frame mounting points and chosen M5 hardware.
  • Use free-body diagrams and beam and buckling calculations to identify the main load path and bolt reactions.
  • Run simple FEA cases for vertical payload, side-to-side sway, and asymmetric top loading.
  • Bench test vertical deflection and overload strength with test stock.
  • Ride the rack on local gravel, then inspect fasteners, bracket interfaces, and any fatigue or movement.

Manufacturing Plan

Frame fit

Measure the bike, tire envelope, heel clearance, seatpost area, and rear mounting points before finalizing the CAD. Make a wood or cardboard mock-up.

Refine details

Refine fit and design. Design joints so geometry carries side load where possible, instead of relying only on threads and bolt preload.

Machine brackets and flat/angle members from available stock so the first version can be built quickly and revised cheaply. Test static load on beams to confirm hand calculations.

Load it up and go on a ride! Inspect, take notes, use data to improve the next version.

Design References

The rack is loosely inspired by simple, compact bikepacking racks rather than traditional touring racks. The references I am looking at include the Tailfin racks, the Mica Rattail rack, the Relentless Mule rack, and the Restrap Switch rack. The design direction I like most is a compact rack that behaves almost like a very rigid saddle bag support, with the option to add small side bags only if the structure and frame mounts allow it.

Takeaway

I'm building this partly to get myself a cheap bikepacking rack, and partly to improve my design skills, hand calculation, and CNC milling skills. I've also had to make hard design choices based on priorities. A bent rack would probably be cleaner, and a production version would justify better bending or tube-forming tooling. For a one-off rack prioritizing cost and using materials already available to me, I'm choosing 6061 and a bolted assembly.
